Festivals in Maharashtra




Ganesh Chathurthi

Lord Ganesha, the deity of wisdom, is the most loved in Maharashtra and Ganesh Chaturthi is celebrated with the most grandeur. In August, preparations for the auspicious day when Lord Ganesh was born – begin with great enthusiasm. It is an 11-day festival, beginning with the installation of beautifully sculpted Ganesh idols in homes and large pandals, colorfully decorated, depicting religious and cultural themes. T he Ganesh idols are worshiped and many cultural events are organised. People throng the huge pandals to catch a glimpse of their favorite God. Visits to friends and family to offer prayers and join the celebrations, and partake some delicious food are looked forward to by all. It is also a great chance to gorge on modaks (steamed rice-flour dumplings with a succulent coconut and ghee filling), since they are considered to be the Lord’s favorite food. On the 11th day comes the time to bid farewell to Ganesha.People take the idols in procession with an accompaniment of music and dance for immersion in the sea or nearby river or lake.



Pola

Pola is a vibrant festival celebrated across rural areas in Maharashtra to show gratitude to the hardworking bulls that support farmers in the fields. On this day, bulls are bathed, decorated with colorful ornaments, painted horns, and garlands. Farmers worship them as a symbol of strength and prosperity. Children join the celebrations by making clay bull models, and processions fill the streets with joy and music. Pola is more than a festival — it’s a heartfelt tribute to the bond between the farmer and his companion, reminding us of the deep roots of rural tradition and respect for nature.





Gudi Padwa

Gudi Padwa is a major festival celebrated across Maharashtra as the Marathi New Year. It falls on the first day of the Hindu month of Chaitra (March–April) and marks the beginning of spring, harvest, and a fresh cycle of life. The festival symbolizes joy, prosperity, and victory. The highlight of Gudi Padwa is the hoisting of a Gudi — a bamboo stick adorned with a bright silk cloth, neem leaves, mango leaves, a garland, and a silver or copper pot on top. This is placed outside the house or at the window and is believed to bring good fortune and ward off evil. People clean their homes, draw colourful rangoli, and wear traditional clothes. Special dishes like Puran Poli, Shrikhand, and Neem-jaggery chutney are prepared, representing the sweet and bitter flavors of life. Many local communities celebrate with cultural programs, traditional Lezim and Dhol-Tasha performances, and festive gatherings. Gudi Padwa reflects the spirit of renewal and pride in Maharashtrian culture. It is a time for families to come together, offer prayers, and welcome a new year filled with hope and positivity.





Diwali

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most grandly celebrated festivals in Maharashtra. Spanning five days, it symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Homes are cleaned, decorated with beautiful rangolis, flowers, and rows of oil lamps (diyas) to welcome positivity and prosperity. The festival begins with Vasu-Baras, followed by Dhanteras, Narak Chaturdashi, Lakshmi Pujan, Padwa, and ends with Bhaubeej, which celebrates the bond between brothers and sisters. On Lakshmi Pujan, people worship Goddess Lakshmi for wealth and happiness, and light crackers to express joy. Families wear new clothes, exchange sweets, and prepare special traditional dishes like chakli, karanji, anarse, and shankarpale. Markets glow with festive decorations, lanterns (akash kandils), and diya stalls. Children eagerly participate in bursting firecrackers and enjoying the festive treats. Diwali in Maharashtra is not just a festival; it's a beautiful blend of tradition, warmth, family bonding, and cultural pride. Each day holds its own meaning, making Diwali a time of deep spiritual joy and celebration.
